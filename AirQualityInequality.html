Air Quality Inequality in America

Income, Air Quality, and Health Across U.S. Counties

This project investigates the correlation between environmental hazards (Air Quality Index), socioeconomic status (Median Income, Demographics), and public health outcomes (Cancer Incidence Rates) at the U.S. county level.

By integrating data from the EPA, U.S. Census Bureau, and CDC, we examine whether lower-income communities face disproportionate exposure to air pollution and what that means for long-term public health.

Overview

Three federal datasets were merged into a SQLite relational database. This allowed us to directly compare air quality, income, and health data for the same counties side by side.

EPA Air Quality

Annual AQI statistics unhealthy air day counts per county.

Census Income

Median household income, poverty rate, and population density from the American Community Survey.

CDC Cancer Rates

Age-adjusted cancer incidence rates per 100,000 people, used as a long-term health outcome.

SQLite Database

All three sources joined for reproducible, structured analysis.

Analysis

Exploratory analysis reveals consistent patterns: as county income decreases, air quality tends to worsen. The tabs below show different angles of this relationship from raw correlations to income quartile comparisons to cancer incidence trends.

Income vs Median AQI

Each point is a U.S. county. The downward trend shows that higher-income counties tend to have lower (better) AQI values.

Key Correlations (Pearson r)

Income is negatively correlated with both AQI and cancer rates. Poverty is positively correlated meaning more poverty, worse air quality.

Median AQI by Income Quartile

Counties in the lowest income quartile average roughly 50% higher AQI than those in the highest quartile.

Unhealthy Air Days per Year

The lowest-income counties average over 18 unhealthy air days per year which is more than four times the rate of the wealthiest counties.

Cancer Rate vs Median Income

A key finding: income correlated more strongly with cancer incidence than AQI alone, suggesting socioeconomic factors compound health risk beyond pollution exposure.

Poverty Rate vs Max AQI

Counties with higher poverty rates experience more extreme pollution peaks, not just higher average AQI.

Random Forest Model

We trained a Random Forest classifier to predict whether a county is pollution-disadvantaged which would be defined as having an AQI above the national median. The model was trained on socioeconomic features and evaluated on a held-out test set.

Key Findings

The analysis confirms a clear, statistically significant relationship between income and air quality at the U.S. county level.

Income and AQI are negatively correlated

Lower-income counties consistently record higher median AQI values. The relationship is statistically significant (p < 0.01).

Income predicts cancer better than AQI alone

Socioeconomic deprivation compounds health outcomes through multiple pathways, not just pollution exposure, but also healthcare access and occupational risk.

Poverty rate is the top model feature

In the Random Forest, poverty rate ranked above median income and population density as the best predictor of pollution disadvantage.

Data sparsity limits inference

Only ~400 counties had complete records across all three sources, which limits statistical power. Merging helped but could not overcome gaps in federal reporting.
